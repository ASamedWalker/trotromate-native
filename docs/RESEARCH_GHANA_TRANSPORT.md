# Ghana Trotro System: Comprehensive Research

*Compiled February 2026. All GHS figures contextualized against prevailing rate: USD 1 ~ GHS 15-16.*

---

## 1. Trotro System Overview

### What Is a Trotro?

A trotro (also spelled tro-tro) is an informal, privately-owned minibus that serves as the backbone of public transit across Ghana. The name derives from the Ga word for "three pence" — the colonial-era fare. The fleet consists of converted vans (Nissan Urvan, Toyota Hiace, Hyundai Grace, KIA Pregio, Mercedes Sprinter, Mazda Bongo) seating 14-25 passengers. Vehicles are not air-conditioned, lack seat belts, and have no formal ticketing — passengers pay the conductor ("mate") in cash.

The system operates on a **fill-and-go model**: vehicles wait at terminals until capacity is reached, then depart. No printed schedules, no advance ticketing, no formal route maps. Knowledge of the network is entirely oral and experiential.

Operational since the late 1950s, trotro became dominant in the 1970s-80s as the State Transport Corporation declined. Stations are economic hubs supporting food vendors, mobile money kiosks, hawkers, and head porters (kayayei).

### Fleet Size

No authoritative public figure exists for total trotro fleet size. The DVLA holds registration data but does not publish vehicle-model-level breakdowns. What is known:
- Roads account for **96% of all internal passenger traffic** in Ghana
- Commercial vehicles account for **60% of passenger movement** nationwide
- The informal transport sector provides approximately **95% of transport services**
- Greater Accra fleet estimated in the tens of thousands; national estimates: 100,000-200,000 commercial passenger vehicles (including taxis)

### Daily Ridership

- **3.5 million passenger trips per weekday** in Greater Accra on trotros alone
- **Over 70%** of daily commuters in Accra rely on informal public transport
- **75%+ of city dwellers** use trotros for commuting (GIZ 2023)
- **85% of Ghanaians** nationwide estimated to use trotros

### How Routes Are Organized

- **No official route maps exist** — routes emerge organically based on demand
- **Terminals (lorry parks)** are the anchors; Circle station (Kwame Nkrumah Interchange) is the largest hub
- Drivers register with a union to operate from a terminal; the union controls route access
- The GUMAP mapping project (2020-2022) found "ghost routes" — lines that appeared in official databases but had stopped running

### Fare-Setting

Fares set by the **Ghana Road Transport Coordinating Council (GRTCC)** in consultation with the Ministry of Transport and unions:
- Approved 2025 fares: **GHS 3-10** for intra-city routes (~$0.20-$0.65)
- **15% fare reduction** implemented May 2025 (fuel price drop)
- **20% fare increase** August 2025 (rising spare parts costs + GHS 1/litre fuel levy)
- The GPRTU distanced itself from the August increase — multiple bodies claim authority, causing confusion

### Transport Unions

1. **GPRTU (Ghana Private Road Transport Union)** — largest and most influential, affiliated with TUC since 1941. Controls majority of routes, negotiates fares with government.
2. **PROTOA (Progressive Transport Owners Association)** — second major union
3. **GCTA (Ghana Co-operative Transport Association)** — smaller player

Unions control terminals, assign drivers, adjudicate disputes. Their resistance to digitization is significant — in 2024, GPRTU stated the government's Tap n' Go system "can never work" in the trotro context.

## 2. Market Size & Economics

### Transport's Share of GDP

- Transport & Storage: **8.4% sectoral GDP growth contribution** in Q4 2024
- Transport, Storage & Communication: **16.56% of GDP composition** (Statista 2023)
- GDP from Transport (nominal): **GHS 2,668 million** in Q2 2024
- Roads carry **96% of all internal traffic**

### Average Spending per Commuter

- Workers earning GHS 2,000-3,000/month spend ~**GHS 440/month on trotro fares** — **15-22% of monthly income**
- Daily commute spend: ~**GHS 15-20/day**
- Some workers spend **20-30% of monthly earnings** on transport
- Traffic congestion costs Ghana an estimated **GHS 4.5 billion annually** ($280M):
  - Time lost: GHS 3.2 billion (71%)
  - Fuel waste: GHS 434 million (10%)
  - Productivity loss from fatigue: GHS 815 million (18%)

### Trotro Industry Revenue (Estimated)

No official figure exists. Conservative bottom-up estimate:
- 3.5M daily trips in Greater Accra x average fare GHS 6 = **~GHS 21M/day**
- Annual (Greater Accra, 300 working days): **~GHS 6.3 billion/year** (~$420M USD)
- National (Kumasi, Tamale, Takoradi, etc.): likely **2-3x Greater Accra**

*Note: This is a modeled estimate, not an audited figure.*

### Driver Economics — The "Target System"

The trotro operates on a **daily franchise model**:
1. Vehicle owner sets a fixed daily fee ("sales") — non-negotiable
2. Driver and mate operate the vehicle
3. From gross revenue: pay daily sales to owner + fuel costs (owner does not contribute to fuel)
4. Remainder split between driver and mate

**Example (pre-fuel increase):**
- Revenue: GHS 700/day → Fuel: GHS 105 → Sales: GHS 170 → **Driver/mate: ~GHS 425/day**

**Post-fuel-increase:**
- Revenue: GHS 840/day → Fuel: GHS 450 → Sales: GHS 170 → **Driver/mate: ~GHS 220/day**

This economic precarity drives dangerous behaviors: overloading, speeding, 12+ hour shifts.

## 3. Key Problems & Pain Points

### Fare Disputes & Lack of Transparency
- Fares not posted visibly; mates quote prices that may not match approved rates
- GPRTU maintains a task force to arrest drivers charging unapproved fares
- Multiple bodies claim authority over fares, creating confusion for passengers and drivers

### No Route Information
- No official printed route maps
- No apps with real-time vehicle locations
- New residents must learn the network by word of mouth
- Even the GUMAP project only achieved ~80% accuracy with dedicated field teams

### Cash-Only Payment
- All fares paid in cash to the mate
- No receipts issued
- Despite 60%+ mobile money penetration, trotros remain entirely cash-based
- Fare theft by mates from owners is a documented problem

### Safety Crisis
- **1,937 road fatalities** in 2024 (full year)
- **7,561 injuries** in first half of 2024 alone
- **16,348 vehicles** involved in crashes in 2024
- Commercial vehicles responsible for ~40% of road fatalities
- Root causes: driver fatigue, overloading, poor maintenance, speed under target pressure

### Long Queues and Wait Times
- Fill-and-go model means 30-60 minute waits during off-peak
- Average commute time in Greater Accra: **58-110 minutes** (for an average distance of only 3.28 km)
- Congestion-induced fatigue reduces productivity by ~30 min/commuter/day

### Overloading
- Vehicles regularly exceed registered capacity; standing passengers common
- Owner pressure (non-negotiable daily targets) creates structural incentive to overload

## 4. Government & Policy

### Aayalolo BRT (Bus Rapid Transit)

Launched November 2016, World Bank-funded. Status: **struggling**.
- Dedicated bus lanes never properly enforced
- USB charging, Wi-Fi, air conditioning have broken down
- Management acknowledged "operational peak" — no further capacity without enforced lanes
- Now described as a cautionary tale about infrastructure lacking institutional enforcement

### State Transport Corporation (STC)

- **Cannot boast 60 fully operational buses** (Minister of Transport)
- Operates mostly inter-regional and some international routes
- Fares: GHS 85-320 domestic, GHS 150+ international
- Government promised procurement; fleet remains critically small

### Government Digital Initiatives — Tap n' Go

Launched February 2024 by then-VP Bawumia:
- Digital card + mobile app for paying public transport fares
- Integrates with Ghana Card, mobile money, Digital Address System
- Kaneshie terminal was first to go cashless

**Critical failure:** GPRTU flatly rejected it: *"It can never work."* As of 2025, Tap n' Go has not been meaningfully deployed across trotros.

### Electric Vehicle Policy

- E-Mobility Policy Working Group inaugurated November 2024
- **8-year import duty exemption** for EVs used in public transport
- VAT exemption for EV imports for public transportation
- 1,957 EVs + 19,000+ hybrids licensed as of 2024
- Government committed: **no new petrol/diesel vehicles after 2045**
- Transport sector: 17% of Ghana's total greenhouse gas emissions

## 5. Technology & Competition

### Existing Apps

| App | What It Does | Status |
|-----|-------------|--------|
| Trotro App (Trufi/OSM) | Route planning using mapped routes. No real-time data. | Active, community-maintained |
| Trotro.Live | Digitizing trotro info for Accra, Kumasi, Obuasi | Early stage |
| Akwaaba App | Multi-modal: taxis, shared rides, trotro | Active |
| Tap & Hop | Tap on/off mobile money fare payment | Pilot stage |
| GO Ride | Government-backed ride-hailing | Status unclear |

**Key insight:** No app has real-time trotro tracking. No app has achieved mass adoption for fare payment. The space is essentially open.

### Uber/Bolt vs Trotro

- **Bolt** leads Ghana ride-hailing: 563,000-667,000 weekly active users
- **Uber**: 227,000-248,000 weekly active users
- Ride-hailing costs **5-10x more than trotro** for the same journey
- They serve entirely different price points — not competitors

### Mobile Money Penetration

- **59.7%** of Ghanaians 15+ have a mobile money account (Jan 2024)
- **MTN MoMo: 17.7 million active users** (Q3 2025)
- MTN commands **89.3% of all Mobile Financial Services revenue** in Ghana
- MoMo revenue: GHS 4.4 billion ($284M)
- Over 19,000 active mobile money agents nationwide

### Smartphone & Internet

- **24.06 million internet users** — **69.8% penetration**
- **38.95 million mobile connections** (113% of population)
- **77% of web traffic** is mobile
- Android is the dominant OS

## 7. What Passengers Want (GIZ 2023 Study)

The GIZ Ghana Mobility Study explicitly identified these as features passengers desire:
1. **Real-time trotro tracking/location**
2. **E-payment capability**
3. **Ability to report driver misbehavior**
4. **Route and trip planning**

None of these are delivered at scale today.

---

## 8. Competitive Moat Analysis

The trotro network is an oral, relationship-based system. Any digital product that encodes this network into structured data (routes, stops, fares, schedules) builds a **proprietary data asset that is very hard to replicate**. The network effects are strong: more users = more reports = better data = more users.
